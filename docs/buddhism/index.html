Buddhism

Buddhism is a religion founded by Siddhartha Gautama, known as the Buddha, in the 5th–4th century BCE in ancient India. It is centered on the teachings of the Buddha, which aim to guide individuals toward liberation from suffering and the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara). The core principles of Buddhism are encapsulated in the Four Noble Truths, which explain the nature of suffering, its causes, and the path to its cessation, and the Noble Eightfold Path, a practical guide to ethical conduct, mental discipline, and wisdom.

Buddhism emphasizes mindfulness, meditation, and ethical living as central practices for personal transformation.

Theravada​

Theravada Buddhism, often considered the oldest school, is grounded in the teachings of the Pali Canon. It emphasizes individual enlightenment through the practices the historical Buddha recommended, including morality (sila), meditation (samadhi), and wisdom (panna). This path, sometimes called the “Path of the Elders,” is prominent in Southeast Asian countries like Thailand, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar. Theravada places significant focus on the monastic community (sangha) and encourages lay practitioners to support and learn from the monastics, who follow the Eightfold Path to attain Nirvana, the cessation of suffering.

Thai Forest Tradition​

The Thai Forest Tradition is a branch of Theravāda Buddhism that emphasizes ascetic monastic practices, meditation, and direct experience of the Buddha’s teachings. Rooted in the wilderness, monks in this tradition often live in forests or remote areas, following a simple and disciplined lifestyle to foster mindfulness and concentration. It prioritizes the cultivation of samatha (calmness) and vipassana (insight) meditation, with less emphasis on scholarly study. Key figures, such as Ajahn Mun and Ajahn Chah, have been instrumental in shaping and popularizing the tradition, which values detachment, mindfulness, and living in harmony with nature as pathways to enlightenment.

Sri Lankan Theravada​

Sri Lankan Theravāda Buddhism is the oldest and one of the most traditional forms of Theravāda, deeply rooted in the Pāli Canon and monastic discipline (Vinaya). It emphasizes scriptural study, meditation, and lay-monastic collaboration, with monastics focusing on preserving the teachings and lay practitioners supporting the Sangha through offerings and ceremonies. Key practices include mindfulness meditation (satipatthāna) and devotional rituals, such as chanting and observing precepts. The Mahāvihāra lineage has been the cornerstone of Sri Lankan Theravāda, influencing other Theravāda traditions across Southeast Asia. Modern revival movements have also emphasized Vipassana meditation and adapting Buddhism to contemporary challenges.

Burmese Vipassana​

Burmese Vipassana is a meditation-focused tradition within Theravāda Buddhism that emphasizes insight meditation (vipassana) as a means to achieve liberation. Developed and popularized in Myanmar, it highlights mindfulness (sati) and deep awareness of the impermanence, suffering, and non-self characteristics of existence. Prominent figures like Ledi Sayadaw, Mahasi Sayadaw, and S.N. Goenka played significant roles in modernizing and spreading Vipassana globally, making it accessible to lay practitioners. Burmese Vipassana methods often involve systematic techniques for observing bodily sensations, mental states, and the flow of experience to cultivate profound insight and detachment. This tradition is central to the global mindfulness movement and remains a cornerstone of modern Theravāda practice.

Mahayana​

Mahayana Buddhism, meaning “Great Vehicle,” is one of the main branches of Buddhism, emerging around the 1st century CE as a reform movement within earlier traditions. It emphasizes the bodhisattva ideal, where practitioners strive to attain enlightenment not only for themselves but for the benefit of all sentient beings. Central to Mahayana is the belief in the universal potential for Buddhahood and the concept of śūnyatā (emptiness), which asserts that all phenomena lack inherent existence.

Mahayana introduced new scriptures, such as the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtras, and developed diverse practices, including devotion to celestial Buddhas and bodhisattvas like Amitabha and Avalokiteśvara. It spread widely across East Asia, adapting to local cultures and giving rise to major schools such as Pure Land, Zen, and Chan. Mahayana remains a vibrant and transformative tradition, emphasizing compassion, wisdom, and the interconnectedness of all life.

Pure Land​

Pure Land Buddhism is a devotional tradition within Mahayana Buddhism that focuses on faith in and devotion to Amitabha Buddha (Amida in Japanese), who vowed to create a Pure Land, or realm of bliss, where beings can be reborn to attain enlightenment. The practice centers on chanting Amitabha’s name (known as nianfo in Chinese or nembutsu in Japanese) as a means of securing rebirth in the Pure Land. This approach simplifies Buddhist practice, making it accessible to laypeople who may find monastic discipline or advanced meditation challenging.

Pure Land Buddhism emphasizes the compassion of Amitabha and the idea that liberation can be achieved through trust in his vow rather than through one’s own efforts. It flourished in China and Japan, with distinct schools like Jodo-shu and Jodo Shinshu developing unique interpretations. Pure Land remains one of the most popular forms of Buddhism in East Asia due to its universal appeal and focus on spiritual inclusivity.

Jodo-shu​

Jodo-shu, or the “Pure Land School,” is a Japanese Buddhist tradition founded by Honen in the 12th century. It emphasizes the exclusive practice of chanting Amitabha Buddha’s name (nembutsu) as the sole path to rebirth in the Pure Land, where enlightenment is assured. Honen’s teachings simplified Buddhist practice, focusing on reliance on Amitabha’s compassion rather than self-powered efforts like meditation or ascetic practices.

Jodo-shu highlights the idea of tariki, or “other-power,” meaning liberation is achieved through Amitabha’s vow rather than through individual merit or effort. This made Buddhism more accessible to common people in Japan, particularly those who found monastic practices or intellectual study daunting. Honen’s approach set the foundation for subsequent Pure Land movements, including Jodo Shinshu, and remains an enduring spiritual tradition.

Jodo Shinshu​

Jodo Shinshu, or the “True Pure Land School,” was founded by Shinran, a disciple of Honen, in 13th-century Japan. It represents a further development of Pure Land Buddhism, emphasizing absolute reliance on the compassion and vow of Amitabha Buddha (Amida). Shinran taught that salvation comes solely through Amitabha’s “other-power” (tariki) and rejected the efficacy of self-powered (jiriki) practices such as asceticism or multiple repetitions of the nembutsu.

Instead, Jodo Shinshu focuses on deep, sincere faith (shinjin) in Amitabha’s vow, with the nembutsu (“Namu Amida Butsu”) seen as an expression of gratitude rather than a practice for accruing merit. It is a deeply inclusive tradition, welcoming all people regardless of social status or personal ability to engage in traditional Buddhist practices. Jodo Shinshu became one of the most influential Buddhist schools in Japan and continues to thrive today, particularly among lay communities.

Chan​

Chan Buddhism is a Chinese tradition that emphasizes meditation (dhyana) and direct insight into the nature of reality, rather than reliance on scripture or ritual. Emerging around the 6th century CE, Chan traces its origins to the legendary Indian monk Bodhidharma, who is said to have introduced meditation-focused practice to China. Central to Chan is the idea of awakening through direct experience, often using methods like koans (paradoxical riddles) or silent sitting to transcend conceptual thinking and attain insight.

Chan places great importance on simplicity, mindfulness, and living in harmony with the natural flow of life. While deeply rooted in Mahayana teachings, it often diverges from traditional doctrinal study, focusing instead on the immediacy of enlightenment. Chan later spread to other East Asian countries, evolving into Zen in Japan, Seon in Korea, and Thiền in Vietnam, becoming one of the most influential forms of Buddhism in the region.

Seon​

Seon Buddhism is the Korean adaptation of the Chan tradition, emphasizing meditation and direct experience of enlightenment. Emerging in Korea during the 7th century, Seon blends the meditative focus of Chan with elements of traditional Korean Buddhism, including devotion to bodhisattvas and ritual practices. Seon stresses the practice of hwadu (critical phrases or questions, similar to koans) as a tool to cut through delusion and realize one’s true nature.

While rooted in the Mahayana teachings of emptiness and compassion, Seon emphasizes simplicity and the immediacy of awakening over scholastic study. It played a key role in shaping Korean spirituality and culture, becoming one of the dominant forms of Korean Buddhism. Modern Seon continues to thrive, balancing traditional monastic practice with outreach to lay practitioners.

Yunmen​

The Yunmen school is a branch of Chan Buddhism, founded by the Chinese master Yunmen Wenyan during the late Tang and early Song dynasties. Known for its sharp and concise teachings, the Yunmen school emphasizes direct and immediate insight into the nature of reality, often employing brief, enigmatic statements or responses to provoke a breakthrough in understanding. Yunmen’s teachings frequently include a single word, phrase, or metaphor to challenge students to transcend ordinary conceptual thinking.

This school highlights the importance of intuitive wisdom and spontaneity, encouraging practitioners to experience enlightenment directly rather than through intellectual analysis or prolonged doctrinal study. While the Yunmen school eventually merged with other Chan traditions, its style and methods have left a lasting influence on Chan and Zen practice.

Fayan​

The Fayan school is a Chinese Chan Buddhist tradition established during the late Tang and early Song dynasties by the master Fayan Wenyi. Known for its intellectual and doctrinal approach, the Fayan school emphasized harmony between meditation practice and the study of Buddhist teachings. It integrated insights from Mahayana philosophy, particularly those related to emptiness (śūnyatā) and interdependence, with the Chan emphasis on direct experience.

Unlike some other Chan schools that relied on spontaneous or enigmatic teaching methods, the Fayan school placed greater importance on logical discussion and gradual understanding. Its teachings often used metaphors and structured dialogues to guide students toward awakening. Though the Fayan school eventually merged with other Chan lineages, its emphasis on synthesizing meditation and doctrine influenced the broader development of Chan Buddhism.

Zen​

Zen Buddhism is the Japanese form of Chan, emphasizing meditation (zazen) and direct, experiential insight into the nature of reality. Zen de-emphasizes reliance on scripture and doctrinal study, focusing instead on disciplined practice and the cultivation of mindfulness and awareness in everyday life. It places great importance on simplicity, spontaneity, and a non-conceptual approach to awakening.

Zen is traditionally divided into several schools, including Rinzai, which uses koans (paradoxical questions) to spark sudden enlightenment (satori); Soto, which emphasizes shikantaza (“just sitting”) as a meditative practice; and Obaku, which integrates Zen with Pure Land devotional elements. Zen became influential in Japanese culture, inspiring art, architecture, tea ceremony, and martial arts. Its emphasis on living in the present moment and transcending conceptual thinking has made it a globally recognized and practiced form of Buddhism.

Rinzai​

Rinzai Zen is a school of Japanese Zen Buddhism known for its emphasis on kensho, or sudden enlightenment, as a transformative experience. Founded by the Chinese master Linji (Rinzai in Japanese) and later brought to Japan by Eisai in the 12th century, Rinzai uses dynamic methods such as koan practice—paradoxical questions or statements—to push practitioners beyond conventional thinking and into direct insight.

Rinzai also incorporates strict monastic discipline, physical activities, and intense meditation (zazen) to prepare the mind for awakening. This school values spontaneity, energy, and a direct approach to realization, often employing sharp language or actions to jolt students out of their attachments. Rinzai has deeply influenced Japanese culture, particularly in the arts, such as calligraphy, tea ceremony, and martial arts, reflecting its aesthetic of simplicity and focus.

Soto​

Soto Zen is the largest school of Japanese Zen Buddhism, emphasizing shikantaza, or “just sitting,” as its primary meditative practice. Introduced to Japan by the monk Dogen in the 13th century, Soto focuses on silent sitting and the cultivation of awareness in the present moment, encouraging practitioners to embody enlightenment in their daily lives rather than striving for sudden awakening.

Unlike the Rinzai school, Soto places less emphasis on koans and dramatic breakthroughs, advocating a more gradual and inclusive approach to spiritual development. Dogen’s teachings highlight the unity of practice and enlightenment, asserting that the act of sitting itself is an expression of awakening. Soto Zen is deeply rooted in simplicity, mindfulness, and the integration of meditative awareness into all aspects of life, making it a widely practiced and influential tradition both in Japan and internationally.

Obaku​

Obaku Zen is a Japanese school of Zen Buddhism that blends traditional Zen practices with elements of Pure Land Buddhism. It was founded in the 17th century by the Chinese monk Ingen Ryuki, who brought practices and teachings from the Chinese Linji (Rinzai) tradition to Japan. Obaku Zen emphasizes a combination of seated meditation (zazen), chanting, and devotional practices, including the recitation of Amitabha Buddha’s name, reflecting its integration of Pure Land influences.

Distinctive features of Obaku include its Chinese-style rituals, architecture, and use of vernacular Chinese in ceremonies, setting it apart from the more Japanized Rinzai and Soto schools. Obaku retains a focus on disciplined meditation and koan practice while incorporating chanting and devotional elements as pathways to enlightenment. Though smaller in size compared to Rinzai and Soto, Obaku remains a significant tradition, celebrated for its synthesis of Zen and Pure Land practices.

Vajrayana​

Vajrayana, or the “Diamond Vehicle,” is a branch of Buddhism that emphasizes esoteric practices, tantric rituals, and the use of skillful means (upaya) to achieve enlightenment. It evolved from Mahayana Buddhism, incorporating elements of Indian tantra, and is considered the fastest path to awakening, often described as a way to achieve Buddhahood in a single lifetime. Vajrayana focuses on direct, transformative practices that utilize visualization, mantra recitation, ritual, and meditation to cultivate profound realization and transcend ordinary perceptions.

Central to Vajrayana is the idea of the guru or teacher, who provides initiation (empowerment) and guides practitioners through complex meditative techniques. It also emphasizes the transformation of ordinary experiences into spiritual opportunities through the understanding of emptiness (śūnyatā) and the nature of mind. Vajrayana is primarily practiced in Tibet, Mongolia, Bhutan, and parts of Japan (e.g., Shingon), and its vibrant traditions include artistic expressions like mandalas and thangkas that symbolize the path to enlightenment.

Tibetan​

Tibetan Buddhism is a form of Vajrayana Buddhism practiced primarily in Tibet, Bhutan, Nepal, Mongolia, and Himalayan regions. It incorporates Mahayana teachings with tantric rituals, esoteric practices, and a focus on the guru-disciple relationship. Tibetan Buddhism emphasizes meditation, visualization of deities, mantra recitation, and the use of mandalas as tools for spiritual transformation. It also includes elements of earlier Tibetan spiritual traditions, such as Bön, blending them into a distinct religious system.

Tibetan Buddhism is divided into four major schools: Nyingma, Kagyu, Sakya, and Gelug, each with unique teachings and practices but sharing the common goal of achieving enlightenment for the benefit of all beings. Central to the tradition is the concept of the tulku system, such as the Dalai Lama, where spiritual leaders are believed to reincarnate to guide others. Tibetan Buddhism is renowned for its detailed philosophical systems, emphasis on compassion, and rich ritual culture, making it one of the most vibrant forms of Buddhism.

Nyingma​

The Nyingma school, meaning “Ancient School,” is the oldest of the four main schools of Tibetan Buddhism, tracing its origins to the 8th century with the introduction of Buddhism to Tibet by Padmasambhava (Guru Rinpoche) and Śāntarakṣita. Nyingma emphasizes the teachings of Dzogchen, or “Great Perfection,” a profound meditative practice aimed at realizing the innate, luminous nature of the mind.

The Nyingma tradition divides its teachings into nine vehicles, with the highest being the Atiyoga teachings of Dzogchen. It values a blend of monastic and lay practice, with practitioners engaging in tantric rituals, visualization, and mantra recitation. Nyingma also preserves many unique texts and practices, including hidden treasures (terma) revealed by tertons (treasure discoverers). Known for its emphasis on direct experience and non-duality, Nyingma has had a lasting influence on Tibetan spirituality and culture.

Kagyu​

The Kagyu school of Tibetan Buddhism, known as the “Oral Lineage,” emphasizes meditation practice and direct experiential insight. It originated in the 11th century, founded by Marpa the Translator, who brought key teachings from India, and his disciple Milarepa, one of Tibet’s most revered yogis. The Kagyu tradition focuses on Mahamudra (“Great Seal”), a meditative practice that aims to directly realize the nature of mind and reality, and the Six Yogas of Naropa, advanced tantric techniques.

Kagyu teachings are transmitted through a strong guru-disciple relationship, with great importance placed on the personal guidance of a qualified teacher. The lineage has several sub-schools, the most prominent being the Karma Kagyu, known for its tradition of the Karmapa reincarnations, and the Drikung and Drukpa Kagyu. Kagyu is celebrated for its emphasis on retreat-based practice, simplicity, and devotion, making it a dynamic and influential force in Tibetan Buddhism.

Sakya​

The Sakya school is one of the four major traditions of Tibetan Buddhism, founded in the 11th century by Khön Könchok Gyalpo. Its name, meaning “Gray Earth,” derives from the unique grayish soil of the region where its main monastery was established. Sakya combines tantric practices with rigorous scholasticism, emphasizing the study of philosophy, logic, and ethics alongside meditative and ritual practices.

The hallmark teaching of the Sakya school is the Lamdre (“Path and Fruit”), a systematized path that integrates sutra and tantra, showing how the foundational practices lead directly to the realization of tantric teachings. Sakya has historically been influential in Tibetan politics and religion, particularly during the 13th and 14th centuries when its leaders governed Tibet under Mongol patronage. Today, it remains a prominent tradition, known for its balanced approach to study, meditation, and ritual practice.

Mongolian​

Mongolian Buddhism is a form of Vajrayana Buddhism heavily influenced by Tibetan Buddhism, particularly the Gelug school, introduced during the 16th century. It became the dominant spiritual tradition in Mongolia after the conversion of Altan Khan, who formalized ties with the Tibetan spiritual leader, the Dalai Lama. Mongolian Buddhism incorporates Tibetan tantric practices, rituals, and texts, often blending with local shamanistic beliefs and customs.

Central practices include meditation, mantra recitation, rituals involving protective deities, and the veneration of reincarnated lamas (tulku system). Monasteries have historically been centers of learning and culture, preserving Buddhist texts, art, and traditions. Despite periods of suppression, particularly during the Soviet era, Mongolian Buddhism has undergone revival since the 1990s and remains a key cultural and spiritual force in modern Mongolia.

Japanese Shingon​

Japanese Shingon Buddhism, meaning “True Word,” is a form of esoteric Vajrayana Buddhism introduced to Japan in the 9th century by the monk Kukai (known posthumously as Kobo Daishi). Shingon emphasizes the use of mantras, mudras (ritual hand gestures), and mandalas (sacred diagrams) to achieve enlightenment. Its teachings are based on the Mahavairocana Sutra and the Vajrasekhara Sutra, with a focus on realizing one’s innate Buddha-nature through esoteric practices.

Central to Shingon is the concept of Sokushin Jobutsu, or “attaining Buddhahood in this very body,” highlighting the immediacy of enlightenment through ritual and meditation. The tradition is deeply ritualistic, involving elaborate ceremonies, fire offerings (goma), and visualizations of cosmic Buddhas like Mahavairocana. Shingon remains influential in Japan, with its headquarters at Mount Koya, a significant pilgrimage site and spiritual center. Its rich rituals and profound philosophical teachings continue to attract practitioners and scholars alike.
